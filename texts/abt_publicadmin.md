On Public Service - Pt. 1

I am about to turn thirty. This date mark comes with a lot of stigma attached to it. 30 was the age my mom was when she had me. 
30 is the age some of my bosses were when I had just joined the public sector. Formative figures in my life and my carreer. 
For me, I don't know very well what this date marker means. In some respects I feel like an accomplished individual. 
I have overcome great obstacles, some have to do with addiction, some with social expectations like graduating, getting a degree. 
I moved out of my home when I was only 23 but it was when I turned 26 that I started to become fully independent.
These were significant milestones for me at the time and, although I have changed and grown and outgrown several of my onceprecious now not so much milestones
I feel all the more impatient and lost starting this new chapter in my life.

I am, as of May 2026, unemployed, disinterested in most of the conversation I have on a regular basis. I feel defeated in every sense of the word and lost beyond
what words can describe. I guess I never really had a concrete plan for what I wanted in my life. The things I used to aspire to, such as independence, prestige,
respect from my peers and from my family, new friends and an exciting job have all come into my life and just the same changed my way of looking at life.

There is something to be said about the hedonic threadmill and breaking up into new barriers of life. I remember I used to be very self aware about my cloathing, 
so I ended up buying more than I could ever need. I wanted to fill some bottomless hole inside me by purchasing my way out of several complexes. In the end I
now am more comfortable wearing my most worn down attire because I hate the prejudice that comes with me dressing up or down any which way. 

I used to want nothing more than a place where me and my friends could hang out, and I built up an appartment with one of my best friends where people anytime 
could come and take a breather from the stresses of everyday life. 

I wanted to be able to tame my PTSD and drug-indused anxieties and after 3 or so years in therapy with heavy downs and slight ups, I am now more in control of myself. 

I wanted my parents to be proud of me and ended up reframing the way I expected this to turn out. In the end, it came out just fine and now I can call my mom 
or dad anytime, even if I wish sometimes there was more support from them, I have managed to humanize them in ways which sometimes have brought me to tears 
thinking about how much I loved them. 

I ran a marathon some years back and have found it in me that I have the guts and the hability to change my life.

Time and time again I have set my life ablaze and come out the other end a better man, but there are only so many times I can do this in a lifetime.

I came into the public sector in a time of need. I was heavily distraught because of my addiction recovery, it was 2021 so the pandemic was still going, 
I remember there had been an initial push for vaccination in the US and people were going abroad to get vaccinated. I was not one of them. I waited for the rollout 
in Mexico which was on September 2021 for me. Gosh we were so ignorant back then I remember people were being classist even about the fucking brand of the vaccine. 

Anyway.

I have always been grateful for this opportunity because it not only helped me get babck on my feet and get a resemblance of a normal life again, but it introduced me
to something I had always loved from afar, which was being a public officer. 

I have written these lines so many times it feels unfair to be on them over and over again, but I'll say it once more. I come from a family of public servants. 
Both, my mom and dad, were the first of their families to go to college. It was public college. And though their jobs in the public sector raised a family, elevated
their standards of living, and gave me and my sister everything. 

Both of them were always incredibly proud that they managed to do this. Both loved and were grateful for their jobs, and I believe that the comofrtable retirement
and lifestyle we are fortunate enough to uphold are proof that through hard work, dedication and a lot of endurance and pain, one can overcome extreme hardships
with the promise of a life worth living. 

My mom, although she has forgotten most of it by now, always used to complain about how the government was corrupt and fucked up and how this country needed people
who were ready to sacrifice their lives for it. To them, Mexico provided them with the tools needed to improve their material conditions. Gave them social security, 
gave them a pension. In short, gave them a shot at a fair life. A comfortable, working class, Mexican dream. 

It was not without it's hardships, but by the time I turnt 20 I had visited Europe thrice, lived in Australia for 6 months, got to know the US along NYC and Texas, 
visited ~20 states of the country and had recieved what most would consider the best education this country has to offer. All private, all on the government's dime. 

I was always beyond grateful to this country. I grew to love it, although I did not know it, I though I did. Now, _alta traición_ by Pacheco rings in my head only
thinking about this. 

It may be romantic and idiotic, but it is the truth as far as I am able to see it. 

And I wanted to take part in it.

I've grown to despice the word intelligence, as it encompassess an enormous range of skills, wits, character traits and even antisocial behaviours in our sad, 
mexican society. Growing up I used to be lauded for my intelligence on the basis of ridiculous things, such as my ability to remember specifics about cars
or football stats from a young age. In school I was lauded because of knowing a series of random facts such as which was the second army that helped defeat the 
Porfirio Diaz dictatorship (División del Norte) or how to compute n^0). 
There's this thing with the changing of scenaries where intelligence starts to be defined in more abstract ways, such as the ability to read and undestand difficult texts, 
to be able to speak a foreign language. These are all skills that one can aquire, but I guess smarts in this sense starts to become the speed whith which we can 
aquire these abilities. In my mind these all come down to the way these are presented to us, and YES, I think about Einstein's "If you judge a fish by its ability
to climb a tree it will live its whole life believing that it's stupid". But there is something to be said about the way to "be smart" in public service. 

This is all a long preamble just to state that I am very stupid, but let me elaborate on this. 

I am, at heart, an idealist. I think about morality and principles in my actions and my catholic background has resurfaced since 2021 by reasons I will not keep
restating. I believe, and have always believed, in the constructive power of art and the way our attention rules our world, like Rilke when he states that beauty
is everywhere, and if a poet is unable to see this, it is because of the poet, not because of life's lack of it. I was thought about principles and morality with 
passion and dedication and have seen the fruits of such labour in a land so dry of them I have come to laud them as the only fortune that perhaps I will every posess. 

But when entering the public service, it was within a year or so, that I sensed for the first time that I was being stupid. This was not the first time in my life
where my stupidity has gotten me into trouble. In school, in my relationships, this sad little defect of mine of being unable to stand the harshness of a life 
lived in fear, in deceit and lies. I have loved Hamlet and The Idiot stands as my favourite work of literature, yes, I am so very predictable. 

Anyhow. What I came here to say is that, it was not until I entered public service that I finally understood a different type of intelligence. One rooted in the cynical
interplay of power and deceit, in lies, robbery, the creation of narratives and a paranoia so entrenched in the players of the game, that I knew, I always knew, 
I just didn't stand a chance. Mind you, I never was an elected officer, I was just a low tier, which came up into a mid tier officer. 

But my god, does the interplay of power stretch deep.
